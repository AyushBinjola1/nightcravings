Product Foundation Document — v1.0

NightCravings
The convenience store already inside your hostel.

A foundation document for a hostel quick-commerce platform, built to feel as trustworthy as the person who already runs it — and to scale from one room to one country.

Prepared for Founding team Status Pre-development Date 11 July 2026
01

Product Vision

Every hostel already has an unofficial "dabba guy" — the room down the hall that quietly stocks Maggi, Lay's, and cold Coke for the 1am crowd. It works because it's fast, known, and near. It fails because it's undiscoverable, cash-only, unpredictable in stock, and invisible until you already know the room number.

NightCravings is that room, made legible. Not a food-delivery app shrunk down for students — a different category: proximity commerce, where the entire fulfillment radius is one building and the entire promise is "what you want, in the time it takes to walk downstairs." We are not competing with Swiggy or Zomato on selection. We are competing with them on the fact that they were never designed for a 40-meter delivery radius, and it shows in their price, their wait time, and their ceremony.

The long-term shape of the company is a network of these rooms — one per hostel block, then one per campus, then a national layer of hyper-local inventory nodes running on one ordering, payments, and operations platform. The website is the first proof that this can feel like a real company and not a WhatsApp order to a warden's nephew.

North star: a student should be able to think "I want something," open the site, and be drinking/eating it in under ten minutes — with zero moments of doubt about whether the order went through.
02

Product Philosophy

These are load-bearing rules, not aspirations — every screen in this document is checked against them.

  1. Speed over complexity — a faster ugly flow beats a slower beautiful one; we just don't have to choose.
  2. Beautiful without being distracting — restraint is the aesthetic, not an afterthought.
  3. Every animation must justify its own existence in milliseconds of user benefit, or it's cut.
  4. Every tap acknowledges itself immediately — state changes are felt, not just eventually visible.
  5. Never overwhelm — one primary action per screen, always identifiable in under a second.
  6. Never require unnecessary thinking — defaults are pre-chosen; the obvious next step is the biggest button.
  7. Everything feels premium — premium is a function of consistency and restraint, not gradients.
  8. Everything feels trustworthy — status is always visible; nothing is ever "probably fine."
  9. Every page nudges toward one more relevant item, never toward an irrelevant one.
  10. Less friction is a revenue lever, not just a UX nicety — treat it with the same rigor as pricing.

05

UX Strategy & the Complete Journey

The problem the journey has to solve: canteens close by 9–10pm, Swiggy/Zomato charge urban-delivery economics for a 40-meter trip and take 30–45 minutes to do it, and there is currently no version of "the hostel shop" that a fresher can find without asking around. Every stage below is designed against that specific gap.

  1. Opening the site
    EmotionNeutral-to-skeptical — "is this actually stocked / actually open right now?"
    OutcomeOpen/closed state and delivery estimate visible before any scrolling.
  2. Landing
    Friction riskMarketing copy where a product grid should be.
    FixHome is the shelf — categories and "your usual" above the fold, zero hero banner.
  3. Browsing
    EmotionLow-effort scanning, often one-handed while lying down.
    OutcomeThumb-reachable grid, sticky category rail, no accidental navigation.
  4. Searching
    Friction riskZero-result dead ends ("Maggi" vs "Instant Noodles").
    FixSynonym-aware search; a miss always falls back to the closest category, never a blank page.
  5. Product discovery
    OutcomeCross-sell is contextual ("goes with Maggi": egg, cheese) — never generic "you may also like."
  6. Product detail
    EmotionShould barely register as a "page" — a sheet, not a navigation.
    OutcomeAdd-to-cart reachable without opening detail at all, for repeat items.
  7. Cart
    Friction riskSurprise costs at the end (a known killer of trust in this exact category).
    FixFinal total shown from the first item added, not revealed at checkout.
  8. Checkout
    OutcomeRoom number is the only address field that exists — no street, city, pincode theatre.
  9. Payment
    EmotionMomentary doubt — "did that actually go through."
    FixUPI-first, single tap, and a state that never leaves the user unsure for more than a second.
  10. Verification
    OutcomeSilent — phone number already known from first order, no re-typing OTPs on repeat visits.
  11. Order confirmed
    EmotionRelief — this is the moment trust is either earned or lost.
    OutcomeImmediate confirmation with a real ETA, not a spinner.
  12. Delivery
    Friction riskSilence during the wait breeds anxiety even at 8 minutes.
    FixA visible status (packed → on the way → at your door) — no GPS map theatre for a 40m walk, just honest steps.
  13. Delivered
    OutcomeOne-tap confirmation the resident received it; this closes the loop the owner needs operationally.
  14. Order history → repeat purchase
    OutcomeEvery past order becomes a one-tap "order again" — this is the single highest-leverage screen for repeat rate.

Information Architecture

Flat by design — a hostel's catalogue is a few hundred SKUs, not tens of thousands. Depth is the enemy of a 30-second order.

Home
├── Status bar          (open/closed · ETA · hostel block selector)
├── Your Usual          (reorder shortcut, personalized, top of scroll)
├── Categories           (Snacks · Beverages · Instant Food · Chocolates · Daily Essentials · Late-Night)
│   └── Category → Product Grid → Product Sheet (in-place, not a route change)
├── Search               (synonym-aware, always one tap away)
├── Cart                 (persistent bottom bar once non-empty)
│   └── Checkout → Payment → Confirmation
├── Orders               (active order tracker + history, each reorderable)
└── Account              (room/hostel, saved payment, support)

Two structural decisions worth stating explicitly: no multi-level category trees (one tap from home to product, always), and the cart is a persistent surface, not a page you navigate away to and lose your place in.

Design Language

The reference points named in the brief translate into concrete rules, not vibes:

ReferenceWhat we take from it
AppleRestraint — one accent color used deliberately, generous negative space, nothing decorative.
LinearDensity done well — compact rows, keyboard-fast interactions, no wasted vertical space on desktop.
Blinkit / ZeptoThe grid-of-products-first home screen, ETA as a first-class UI element, thumb-first mobile layout.
StripeNumeric honesty — prices, totals and timers rendered in tabular figures, aligned, never jumping.
NotionOne consistent component language reused everywhere, so nothing has to be re-learned per screen.

Explicitly avoided: gradient hero banners, heavy drop-shadow cards, generic Material ripple, Bootstrap-blue defaults, admin-template dashboards for the owner console. The brand should never look like a weekend hackathon project selling snacks — nor like a VC-funded app performing seriousness it hasn't earned. The tone is closer to a well-run neighbourhood store that happens to have a beautiful sign.

Color System

The palette reads as a warm, tungsten-lit shop counter in light mode, and the same shop after midnight in dark mode — not an inverted copy of itself. Amber (turmeric, spice, the physical goods) is the action color; indigo (the actual hour most orders happen) is reserved for order-status and time-sensitive UI, so the two accents never compete for the same job.

Paper#FBF7F0
Surface#F3ECE0
Ink#211C16
Accent (amber)#B4520A
Night (status)#2E2A6E
Success#276A45
Warning#966400
Danger#A32626

Dark mode is not a filter over these values — ink becomes a warm off-white (#EEE7DA, never pure white), paper becomes a near-black with a brown cast (#171310, never pure black), and the amber accent is boosted in luminance (#E08A3E) so it stays legible without turning neon. Toggle the artifact's theme switch to see both.

Semantic colors (success/warning/danger) are fixed roles, independent of the two brand accents — an order-status pill is never rendered in raw amber or indigo.

Typography

A serif display face (Charter/Georgia stack) gives the brand a note of an established shop's signage rather than a startup's dashboard; a neutral system-sans carries all functional UI text so nothing competes with product names and prices; tabular figures render every price, ETA and quantity so numbers never visually jitter as they update.

Display / 44NightCravings
Heading / 28Instant Noodles
Subheading / 20Snacks & Chips
Body / 16Delivered to your room in under 10 minutes.
Caption / 13In stock · Block C
Numeric / 18₹149.00

Scale ratio ~1.25 across five steps; line-height 1.1 for display/heading, 1.6 for body; letter-spacing +0.06em on all uppercase labels to compensate for the reduced x-height at that size.

Responsive Strategy

Mobile is the primary — arguably the only truly load-bearing — surface; over 90% of hostel ordering happens on a phone, one-handed, often in bed. Desktop exists mainly for the owner's operations console.

BreakpointRangeLayout behaviour
Compact< 400px2-column product grid, single-hand thumb zone prioritized, cart bar full width.
Standard mobile400–767pxDefault target; 2–3 column grid, sticky category rail.
Tablet768–1023px3–4 column grid, category rail becomes a left column.
Desktop1024–1439pxPersistent sidebar categories + 4-column grid + cart as a side panel, not a bottom bar.
Wide / owner console1440px+Data-dense tables, multi-pane inventory + live order queue.

Foldables and landscape are treated as "just another viewport" rather than special-cased — the grid is fluid (CSS grid + minmax()), never a fixed set of device breakpoints with gaps between them.

13

Accessibility Standards

  • WCAG 2.1 AA minimum contrast on all text/UI, verified against both themes independently — dark mode is not assumed to inherit light mode's ratios.
  • Keyboard: full order flow (browse → cart → checkout) completable without a pointer, for the owner console in particular.
  • Focus states: visible 2px outline on every interactive element, never suppressed for aesthetics.
  • Screen readers: product cards expose name, price, and stock state as one announced unit; cart updates are polite live-region announcements, not silent.
  • Touch targets: minimum 44×44px, following directly from the "thumb, one-handed, sometimes lying down" usage model.
  • Semantic HTML first — ARIA only fills genuine gaps (e.g. custom quantity steppers), never a substitute for correct elements.
  • Reduced motion and reduced data preferences both respected — the latter disables non-essential product imagery preloading.

Performance Standards

Performance is treated as a feature of the "10-minute delivery" promise, not a separate engineering concern — a 3-second product page directly taxes the number on the homepage.

Project Architecture

Structured around one hard constraint: a single hostel today must not be hard-coded anywhere it will need to become "one of many hostels" tomorrow. Every feature folder is scoped by hostelId from day one, even while only one exists.

src/
├── app/                     # Next.js App Router
│   ├── (storefront)/
│   │   ├── page.tsx         # Home — categories + Your Usual
│   │   ├── search/
│   │   ├── cart/
│   │   └── checkout/
│   ├── (account)/
│   │   ├── orders/
│   │   └── profile/
│   └── (owner-console)/     # Separate density, same design tokens
│       ├── inventory/
│       └── order-queue/
├── features/
│   ├── catalogue/           # product/category domain logic + UI
│   ├── cart/
│   ├── checkout/
│   ├── orders/
│   └── delivery-status/
├── components/ui/           # shared primitives (button, sheet, toast…)
├── design-tokens/           # color, type, spacing — single source of truth
├── hooks/
├── services/                # Supabase client, payment gateway adapter
├── stores/                  # Zustand slices (cart, ui)
├── types/
├── lib/                     # zod schemas, formatters, constants
└── config/                  # per-hostel config (hours, delivery radius, fees)

The (owner-console) route group deliberately shares components/ui and design-tokens with the storefront rather than forking its own styles — one design system, two densities.

17

Engineering Standards

  • Every component is typed, accessible by default, and documented with a one-line comment only where behaviour isn't obvious from its name.
  • No magic numbers — spacing, radii, durations and colors are always tokens, never inline literals.
  • No component exceeds ~150 lines before being decomposed; a product card that also contains checkout logic is a smell, not a shortcut.
  • Cart and checkout logic is pure and unit-tested independently of any UI — the one part of the app where a bug is a financial dispute, not a visual glitch.
  • Shared logic is extracted the second it's used twice, not before — no speculative abstractions for a hypothetical third hostel that doesn't exist yet.

20

Product Principles — Distilled

If a proposed feature can't answer "does this get someone from craving to confirmed order faster, or does it deepen trust in a 10-minute promise," it doesn't belong in v1.

Everything in this document — the flat IA, the amber-and-indigo palette, the sub-250ms motion ceiling, the config-driven architecture — exists in service of one sentence: a student should be able to think of something they want, and be holding it before the thought gets old.
