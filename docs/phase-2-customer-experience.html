Customer Experience Specification — Phase 2 · v1.0

The complete NightCravings customer experience.

Builds on the approved Product Foundation Document. This spec defines every screen, state and interaction in the customer-facing web app — not as ecommerce boilerplate, but as a purpose-built late-night experience judged against four numbers: checkout speed, trust, AOV, retention.

Depends on Foundation Doc v1.0 Status Draft for build Date 11 Jul 2026

Design Review — what we challenged before writing this spec

Per brief, every screen and pattern below was tested against one question: does this measurably improve checkout speed, trust, AOV, or retention? Anything that didn't survive was cut or rebuilt. The table is the receipt for that process.

Cut
Fake urgency — "Only 2 left!" banners, countdown-to-discount timers, fake viewer counts. These are the default quick-commerce toolkit and they actively work against trust with a fresher persona who is already unsure if this is a real, credible operation. Replaced with real signals only: true stock counts, true average delivery time, computed live.
Cut
Wishlist / save-for-later as a v1 feature. It optimizes for a browsing behaviour that doesn't exist in this category — nobody "saves" a Maggi for next month. Demoted to Section 11 (Store Closed) as a genuine future "Notify Me," not a permanent nav item.
Added
Verification Heartbeat on the payment-awaiting screen (Section 8). The brief's biggest unsolved trust gap: this business runs on manual UPI verification, so there's an inherent wait with no card network confirming anything automatically. Rather than a spinner, the customer sees a live, honest countdown built from the owner's actual median confirmation time — turning the scariest moment in the flow (did my money just disappear?) into the most reassuring one.
Added
Named recommendation reasons. "Recommended for you" as an unexplained black box reads as manipulative even when it isn't. Every suggestion in this spec carries a visible, true reason — "because you order this every Tuesday," "goes with Maggi," "3 of your floor ordered this tonight" — so upsell reads as helpful, not algorithmic pressure.
Added
Late Night Mode. Not just a dark theme — after the store's evening threshold, Home silently reorders its own sections (Late Night Picks and Your Usual jump above Best Sellers) because the browsing intent at 1am is measurably different from 6pm. One home page, two priority orders, zero extra taps.
Kept, elevated
Payment (Section 8) is written in as much depth as Home, even though the brief names Home the most detailed section. Home is where trust is built; for a manual-UPI, human-verified business, Payment is where trust is proven or broken. Both get full treatment; Home additionally carries the section-by-section "why here" reasoning the brief asked for explicitly.
Full customer flow — every screen below sits on this spine. Nothing branches off it; nothing requires backtracking.
Landing→Home→Search / Browse→Product Sheet→Cart→Checkout→Payment→Awaiting Verification→Confirmed→Delivery Coming→Delivered→History→Reorder
01

Home

Home is not a landing page — "Landing" and "Home" are the same screen for a returning visitor, and even a first-time visitor sees the live shelf within one scroll. The psychology: a hostel resident opening this at 11pm already knows roughly what they want (a craving, not a shopping mission), so Home's only job is to get out of the way between that craving and the cart.

Section-by-section reasoning

  • Top NavigationLogo, hostel-block selector, search icon, cart icon with live count. Nothing else — no mega-menu, because there's nothing under it worth navigating to.
  • Store StatusOpen/closed + live ETA, rendered as the first thing painted (server-rendered, not a client fetch) — the single highest-anxiety question a new visitor has, answered before they can even ask it.
  • Estimated Delivery TimeA real number ("Delivering in ~8 min right now"), computed from the last hour's actual fulfillment times, not a static "10-30 min" range — specificity reads as operational competence.
  • SearchPersistent, one tap from anywhere, because a fraction of visits are pure lookups ("do you have Sprite") with zero interest in browsing.
  • CategoriesSix tiles max, image-led not text-led — this is the "front of the shop" and should be scannable in under two seconds.
  • Your UsualPlaced above Best Sellers for any resident with 2+ past orders — the single highest-conversion, highest-margin row on the entire site, because it removes the browsing step entirely for a repeat need.
  • Continue ShoppingResumes an abandoned cart/session from earlier the same day (e.g. closed the tab mid-browse) — recovers intent that would otherwise be a lost order, shown only when it's genuinely still relevant (same day).
  • Late Night PicksCurated for the post-9pm craving profile (instant food, caffeine, sugar) — see Late Night Mode below. Absent entirely before evening; showing it at noon would be noise, not personalization.
  • Trending TonightA true, live "most-ordered in the last 3 hours across the hostel" row — real social proof (your actual floor, tonight), which is the non-manipulative version of "trending."
  • Best SellersAll-time popularity, for the majority of sessions with no order history yet — a safe, proven default when personalization has nothing to work with.
  • Recommended For YouCollaborative-filter suggestions, but each card is labeled with its actual reason (see Section 12) — never shown as an unexplained "for you."
  • Frequently Bought TogetherLives primarily inside the Product Sheet and Cart (Sections 5 & 6); a light version appears on Home only under Trending Tonight, so Home doesn't turn into a second cart page.
  • Recently OrderedA compact strip distinct from Your Usual — Your Usual is "what you always get," Recently Ordered is "what you got last time," useful when the two diverge (e.g. a one-off bulk order for friends).
  • CombosPre-built bundles at a small effective discount (e.g. Maggi + Egg + Cold Drink) — genuine AOV lever, priced transparently as "3 items, ₹10 off" rather than a manufactured "deal" percentage.
  • Daily EssentialsMilk, bread, eggs — low-emotion, high-frequency items grouped separately from snacks so Kavya (Daily Regular persona) never has to hunt through chocolate categories for curd.
  • New ArrivalsServes the Impulse Buyer persona's actual trigger (novelty) — kept to a single row, never a takeover, since novelty-seeking is a minority behaviour, not the majority use case.
  • FooterSupport contact, store timings, About/Privacy/Terms — present for trust and legal completeness, deliberately unremarkable so it never competes with the shelf above it.

Late Night Mode — a NightCravings-original pattern

Past a configurable evening threshold (default 9:00pm, owner-configurable per hostel), Home doesn't just darken — its row order re-ranks: Late Night Picks and Your Usual move to the top, Daily Essentials and New Arrivals drop below the fold. This is a re-ranking of the same components already on the page, not a different page — so there's no additional route, loading state, or maintenance burden, only a re-ordered data query keyed on time-of-day.

Home

Get a craving into a cart with zero unnecessary steps, while quietly proving the store is real, open, and fast.

UX reasoning

Vertical single-column scroll on mobile (the overwhelming majority surface) — no horizontal-only rows that can be missed, only horizontally-scrolling within a labeled row.

Business reasoning

Row order is a direct AOV lever: Your Usual and Combos are placed to be seen without scrolling on a standard phone viewport (~700px above the fold).

Layout hierarchy

Status bar → Search → Categories → Your Usual → Late Night Picks / Trending → Best Sellers → Recommended → Combos → Daily Essentials → New Arrivals → Footer.

Component hierarchy

StatusBar → SearchBar → CategoryRail → ProductRow (reused 8×, data-driven, one component) → Footer.

User interactions

Tap category → in-place filter, no route change. Tap product card → Product Sheet opens over Home (Home stays mounted underneath). Long-press/hold on a repeat item → quick re-add without opening the sheet.

Animations

Rows fade-and-rise 12px on first paint, staggered 40ms apart, capped at 200ms total — reads as "arrived," never as a loading sequence the user has to wait through.

Loading strategy

Store status and Your Usual are server-rendered (critical path); rows below the fold stream in with skeletons, never blocking first paint.

Empty state

First-ever visit with no order history: Your Usual and Recently Ordered rows are omitted entirely (not shown empty) — Best Sellers takes their visual slot so the layout never looks broken.

Error states

Catalogue fetch failure shows the last successfully cached catalogue (PWA cache) with a small "showing saved menu" notice, rather than a blank page.

Responsive behaviour

Mobile: single column, horizontally-scrolling rows. Tablet: 2-up category grid. Desktop: persistent left category sidebar replaces the horizontal category rail; product rows become fixed 4-column grids.

Accessibility

Each row is a labeled landmark (aria-label="Your Usual"); horizontal scroll rows are keyboard-scrollable with visible focus rings on each card.

Edge cases

Store closes mid-session → status bar updates in place via realtime subscription, non-destructively (cart and scroll position untouched); see Section 11 for the full closed state.

Performance

LCP target is the Store Status bar, not an image — text-based, server-rendered, sub-1.8s even on hostel wifi.

Future scalability

Row composition and order become a per-hostel config object (Section 16 of the Foundation Doc) — a second hostel can reorder or hide rows without a code change.

02

Search

Search exists for the minority of visits that are lookups, not browsing — so its entire design goal is to never dead-end. A catalogue of a few hundred SKUs means search can be exhaustively synonym-mapped by hand; there is no excuse for a zero-result screen in this category.

Search

Get a named craving into the cart in under three keystrokes, and never show a dead end.

Autocomplete

Fires after the 2nd character, client-side fuzzy match against a cached catalogue index — no network round-trip needed for a few hundred SKUs.

Suggestions

Empty query shows Recent Searches (max 5) above Popular Searches (hostel-wide, real) — recent first because it's the higher-intent signal.

Synonym & typo tolerance

Hand-curated synonym map ("maggi"↔"instant noodles", "cold drink"↔"soft drink"↔"soda") plus edit-distance-1 typo correction — both run before falling back to "no exact match."

Category search

Typing a category word ("snacks") returns the category page inline as the top result, not just individual products.

Instant results

Results render per keystroke from the local index; perceived latency is 0ms since nothing leaves the device until Add to Cart.

Loading state

None visible in the common case (local index); a thin top progress bar only if the index itself is still hydrating on a cold load.

Empty results

Never truly empty — worst case falls back to "closest category" plus a direct "Message the store" link, so a genuinely missing item still becomes a conversation, not a dead end.

Search ranking

In-stock first, then personal purchase frequency, then hostel-wide popularity — a rare item you buy weekly should outrank a popular item you've never touched.

Search analytics

Every zero-exact-match query is logged for the owner console — the cheapest, most honest source of "what should I stock next."

Future: voice search

Mic icon reserved in the search bar layout now; deferred until there's evidence residents actually want hands-free search mid-task (e.g. while cooking Maggi).

Keyboard shortcuts

Desktop: / focuses search from anywhere, Esc clears and blurs — a Linear-style convention for the console-adjacent power users (owner + frequent orderers on laptop).

Accessibility

Results announced via aria-live="polite" as they update; full keyboard traversal of the results list with arrow keys.

Performance

Search index (~few hundred rows) is a single small JSON payload cached at the PWA layer — works fully offline, degrades gracefully if network drops mid-search.

03

Category

Categories are a shortcut into the shelf, not a taxonomy to navigate deeply — one tap in, one tap to a product, no sub-category mazes.

Category

Narrow the shelf to what's relevant without ever feeling like a filter form.

Category cards

Photo-led tile, category name, item count — six on Home, always visible, never collapsed behind a "see all."

Category page

Opens in-place (grid re-filters below the same sticky header) on mobile; a genuine route on desktop for shareable/bookmarkable URLs.

Filtering

Only two filters exist: "In stock" (default on) and price range — deliberately not replicating a full ecommerce filter panel this catalogue doesn't need.

Sorting

Default sort is personal-relevance (as in Search); explicit alternatives are Price and Popularity, exposed as a single-tap chip row, not a dropdown.

Sticky category rail

Horizontal on mobile (pinned under the status bar), vertical on desktop (persistent left column) — always shows which category is active.

Desktop

4-column product grid beside a persistent category sidebar; hover reveals the quick-add button without a click.

Mobile

2-column grid, sticky rail scrolls independently from the product grid beneath it.

Animations

Switching category cross-fades the grid (120ms) rather than a hard cut or a slide, since it's a filter change, not a navigation.

Loading

Skeleton grid matching the exact card dimensions, so nothing reflows when real content arrives.

04

Product Card

The card is the single most-rendered component in the app — every pixel on it is either driving a decision or it's being removed.

Product Card

Let a resident decide "yes, add it" without opening anything.

Image

Real photography of the actual stocked item (never a stock/generic packshot) — square, consistent lighting, top 55% of the card. This alone is a trust decision: a fresher recognizing the exact packet on her shelf later builds more confidence than any copy could.

Price

Tabular numerals, bottom-left, always visible without interaction — never hidden behind a "view price" tap.

Stock

Silent when comfortably in stock (absence of a badge is itself the signal); a small "Only 3 left" pill appears only when true and low — real scarcity, never fabricated.

Discount / badges

Single badge slot max, e.g. "Combo," "New" — never stacked badges competing for attention.

Quantity selector

Hidden until the item is already in cart, at which point the Add button morphs into a stepper (− 1 +) in place — no separate quantity UI before commitment.

Add button

Bottom-right, thumb-reachable, filled amber circle with a plus glyph — the single most-tapped target in the app, sized 44px minimum regardless of card size.

Hover (desktop)

Card lifts 2px with a soft border-color shift (no drop shadow); Add button becomes fully opaque from a resting 85% opacity.

Tap (mobile)

Card body tap opens the Product Sheet; Add button tap is a separate hit target that never triggers the sheet — critical so quick re-adds don't accidentally open a detail view.

Animations

Add button press: 2% scale-down + immediate morph to stepper, 150ms. Quantity change: the number cross-fades, never jump-cuts.

Loading

Skeleton is image-block + two grey bars at exact final dimensions — zero layout shift on real content arrival.

Responsive

Card aspect ratio is fixed (1:1.3) across all breakpoints; only the grid column count changes, never the card's internal proportions.

Accessibility

Whole card exposed as one button-labeled region ("Maggi 2-Minute Noodles, ₹18, in stock") for screen readers, with the Add control independently reachable and labeled ("Add Maggi 2-Minute Noodles to cart").

Business reasoning

Every element on this card either speeds up the add-to-cart decision or builds trust in the item being real and available — nothing on it exists to be decorative.

05

Product Sheet

A bottom sheet, never a route — the underlying grid stays mounted and scrolled to the same position when it closes, so opening a product to check something never costs the user their place.

Product Sheet

Answer the one remaining doubt (what exactly is this, how big, how fast) and convert it into an add.

Large image / gallery

Full-width hero photo, swipeable to 1–2 secondary angles for packaged goods where size/pack-count is a real question (e.g. "is this the family pack or single").

Description

One honest sentence, written by a human, not scraped manufacturer copy — e.g. "The 2-minute classic, made fresh in the pantry microwave if you don't have one."

Nutrition / ingredients

Collapsed by default behind a small "Nutrition" toggle — present for the minority who care (dietary restriction, allergy), invisible cost to everyone else.

Preparation & delivery time

Shown together as one line — "Ready to eat in 2 min · delivered in ~8 min" — because for instant food, total time-to-eating is the actual decision driver, not delivery time alone.

Stock

Same honest-only-when-low convention as the card; if genuinely out of stock, Add is replaced by "Notify when back" (Section 11 pattern reused here).

Quantity + Add to Cart

Full-width sticky footer button inside the sheet, so it's reachable without scrolling regardless of how much detail content is above it.

Frequently bought together

2–3 items with a true co-purchase rate, each individually addable with its own checkbox — never a forced bundle.

Recommendations

A single row below FBT, labeled with its real reason (see Section 12), not a second unlabeled "you may also like."

Share

One tap → native share sheet with a deep link straight to this product's sheet, useful for the Wing (friend-group) persona coordinating an order.

Future: reviews

Reserved as a collapsed section once order volume is high enough for reviews to be meaningful signal rather than noise from 1–2 opinions.

Animations

Slides up from the bottom over a dimmed backdrop, 220ms ease-out; dismiss via swipe-down or backdrop tap, tracked 1:1 with the gesture (interruptible, not a fixed-duration animation).

06

Cart

The cart is a persistent surface, not a page you can lose — it exists simultaneously as a mini-bar, a full sheet, and a running total, all reflecting one source of truth.

Cart

Make the running total and the path to checkout impossible to lose track of, while offering one honest nudge toward free delivery.

Sticky mini-cart

A slim bottom bar appears the instant the first item is added — item count, total, and a "View Cart" affordance, present on every screen until checkout.

Cart sheet/page

Bottom sheet on mobile, side panel on desktop (per Foundation Doc Section 11) — never a full route change on mobile.

Free delivery progress

A slim real progress bar — "₹43 more for free delivery" — genuinely true and recalculated live; removed entirely once crossed (replaced by a quiet "Free delivery unlocked" pill, no longer a bar with nothing left to fill).

Recommended products

Shown only below the free-delivery bar, and only while the customer hasn't yet crossed the threshold — the one place a suggestion is explicitly tied to a real, disclosed cost benefit ("add this, hit free delivery").

Undo remove

Removing an item swaps its row for a 4-second "Removed · Undo" state before it actually disappears — protects against an accidental swipe on a crowded bus/bed scenario.

Swipe interactions

Swipe-left on a cart row reveals Remove; swipe never auto-triggers past a hold threshold, avoiding accidental deletes.

Quantity animations

Stepper +/− cross-fades the number and recomputes the total with a brief tabular-number roll, never a hard jump.

Price updates

Total recalculates on every change with zero perceptible delay (pure client-side arithmetic against already-loaded prices) — no spinner ever appears for a quantity change.

Coupons (future)

Field reserved but hidden until a real promotions system exists — an empty, unused coupon input is a worse trust signal than no coupon field at all.

Empty cart

See Section 16 — never a dead end, always a direct route back to Your Usual or Best Sellers.

07

Checkout

Checkout has exactly two required fields — room number and phone — because the entire "address" in this business is a room number. Anything resembling a standard ecommerce address form is actively wrong here.

Checkout

Confirm where and how, in the fewest fields that are physically possible for this delivery model.

Customer details

Room number (required) and phone (required, pre-filled after first order) — that's the complete address model; no city/pincode/landmark fields exist.

Delivery method

Two clear options as large tappable cards, not a dropdown: Room Delivery (₹25, or free above ₹250) and Pickup (₹0, for residents who'd rather grab it themselves) — Pickup exists because forcing delivery on someone one floor away is friction, not service.

Free delivery threshold

₹250, same number shown consistently from the Cart's progress bar through to this screen — no surprise recalculation at the last step.

Payment method

UPI only, selected here, executed in Section 8 — no method choice paralysis (cards/wallets/COD aren't offered because they don't fit a manual-verification, cash-averse operation).

Order notes

Optional single-line field ("no ice," "leave at door") — collapsed behind a "+ Add a note" link so it never adds visual weight to the default path.

Validation

Inline, on blur, never on submit-then-scroll-to-error — a malformed phone number is flagged the moment focus leaves the field.

Errors

Field-level red microcopy stating exactly what's wrong ("Enter a 10-digit number") — never a generic top-of-form banner.

Loading

"Continue to Payment" shows an inline spinner inside the button itself (button stays the same size) while the order draft is created server-side.

Animations

Delivery method cards use a single accent-border highlight on selection, no scale/bounce — checkout is the one place motion is minimized further than the rest of the app, to keep the moment feeling serious and fast.

Accessibility

Delivery method cards are a proper radio group under the hood, fully operable with arrow keys and announced as "Delivery method, 1 of 2, Room Delivery, selected."

08

Payment

This is the single highest-trust-risk screen in the product. There is no payment gateway silently confirming anything — a human on the other end has to see the money land and tap "confirm." Every design decision here exists to make that wait feel safe instead of uncertain.

The eight moments, in order

1 · QR Code

The default path for most UPI apps.

Interaction

Tap to enlarge to near-fullscreen with a soft zoom (180ms) — small QR codes are the #1 real-world scan failure point, so this is the single highest-value micro-interaction on the screen.

Trust detail

The store's verified UPI name is printed directly beneath the code in large type, so the customer can visually confirm they're paying the actual NightCravings account before scanning.

2–3 · UPI ID & Mobile Number

Fallback paths for apps/situations where scanning isn't convenient.

Copy button

One tap copies to clipboard and swaps the button label to "Copied" with a checkmark for 2 seconds — immediate, unambiguous feedback with zero toast/dialog interruption.

Layout

Both shown as secondary, smaller rows beneath the QR — the hierarchy communicates "scan first, these are backups," matching real usage.

4 · Copy Amount

Remove the single most error-prone manual step in a manual-payment flow.

Interaction

The exact order total, tabular numerals, with its own dedicated Copy button right beside it — a mistyped amount is the most common real-world failure in manual UPI, and this removes typing entirely.

5 · Upload Payment Screenshot

Give the owner (and the customer) tangible proof the payment happened.

Interaction

Native file/camera picker; immediate thumbnail preview with a retake/remove option — the customer sees exactly what the owner is about to see, so there's no "did it upload correctly" doubt.

Validation

Required to proceed (this business's only real proof-of-payment); a clear inline reason if the file fails to upload, with automatic retry on flaky hostel wifi.

6 · Transaction ID (optional)

A secondary, faster-to-verify signal for customers whose UPI app surfaces it easily.

Interaction

Optional text field, clearly marked as such — never blocks submission, since not all UPI apps expose this cleanly.

7 · Payment Submitted

Confirm the customer's action landed, distinct from confirming the money landed.

Animation

A single, calm checkmark draw-in (250ms) — deliberately not a confetti/celebration moment, because the order isn't actually confirmed yet and celebrating early would be dishonest.

Copy

"We've got your payment details — confirming now" — states plainly what has and hasn't happened yet.

8 · Awaiting Verification — the "Verification Heartbeat"

Turn an inherent wait into the most reassuring moment in the flow, not the most anxious one.

Interaction

A live status card shows a gentle pulsing indicator plus an honest estimate — "Usually confirmed in under 3 min" — computed from the owner's real rolling median confirmation time, not a fixed promise.

Realtime update

Transitions to Order Confirmed automatically the instant the owner taps confirm (Supabase realtime push) — the customer never has to refresh or wonder.

Escalation path

If the estimate window passes without confirmation, the card itself surfaces a direct "Message the store" action — proactive, not something the anxious customer has to go dig for in Support.

Failure case

If the owner marks payment as not received/mismatched, the customer gets a clear, blame-free explanation and a direct re-upload path — see Section 17.

09

Order Tracking

Four honest stages, each backed by a real state change on the owner's side — never a fake progress animation implying movement that isn't happening.

Order Tracking

Answer "where's my order" before the question forms, for the entire 5–10 minute wait.

Stages

Payment Submitted → Order Confirmed → Delivery Coming → Delivered — a vertical stepper, each stage timestamped as it's reached.

Notifications

Push/web notification fires on every stage change, so the resident doesn't need to keep the tab open (critical for the Gamer persona mid-session).

Animations

Each stage transition draws the connecting line and settles the new active dot with a soft pulse — a single 300ms moment per transition, never continuous motion.

Estimated time

A live countdown appears only from "Delivery Coming" onward — this is a 40-meter walk, not a GPS-tracked vehicle, so a map is deliberately not used; an honest number is more trustworthy than a fake moving pin.

Progress

Persists across app backgrounding/tab close — reopening the site resumes exactly at the current stage, never resets to Home.

History

Every completed order becomes a row in Order History (Section 10) automatically, no separate save action.

Failure cases

Owner-side cancellation (e.g. item actually out of stock after all) triggers an explicit stage — "Order Cancelled" — with a stated reason and an automatic, visible refund-status note, never a silent disappearance.

10

Profile

The least-visited but highest-retention-leverage section — this is where a one-off order becomes a habit.

ItemPurpose
Order HistoryEvery past order, each with a single "Reorder" button that rebuilds the exact cart in one tap — the highest-leverage retention feature in the entire spec.
Saved DetailsRoom number and phone, editable in place — set once, needed nowhere else again.
ThemeLight / Dark / System — a simple three-way switch, respecting whichever the OS already prefers by default.
SupportDirect message/call to the owner — one tap, no ticket system for a business this size.
FAQAnswers the handful of real recurring questions (delivery radius, payment verification time, timings) — kept short enough to actually be read.

11

Store Closed

Closed is not a wall — the catalogue stays fully browsable, because browsing while closed is exactly how tomorrow's order gets decided tonight.

Store Closed

Keep the resident engaged with the catalogue and turn "closed" into a specific, trustworthy reopening moment.

Opening countdown

Replaces the Store Status bar with "Opens in 6h 12m" — same slot, same prominence as the open-state ETA, so the information hierarchy doesn't change, only its content.

Browse catalogue

Fully intact; Add to Cart still works and simply queues the order, clearly labeled "Order will be placed when we open" rather than disabling the whole site.

Notify me (future)

A single opt-in per session — "Notify me when open" — deferred to a real push-notification build, not a fake button that does nothing today.

Store timings

Always visible, one tap from the status bar — removes any guessing about when to come back.

Support

Direct message link stays available even when closed, for genuine emergencies (e.g. a wrong delivery from earlier).

Conversion Optimization

Every technique here is required to disclose its own reasoning to the customer — that single rule is what separates this list from a dark-pattern playbook.

Accessibility

WCAG 2.1 AA across every screen in this document, verified independently in both themes — carried over from the Foundation Doc's standards, applied screen-by-screen here.

  • Every interactive element (card, button, stepper, radio) is reachable and operable by keyboard alone, with a visible focus ring that is never suppressed.
  • Product cards, cart rows, and tracking stages expose their full state (name, price, stock, stage) as one announced unit to screen readers — never split across ambiguous fragments.
  • All copy/success/error feedback is announced via aria-live regions, matching the visual timing exactly.
  • Color is never the only signal — stock, success, and error states pair color with icon and text.
  • Minimum 44×44px touch targets everywhere, following directly from the one-handed, often-lying-down usage model.
  • prefers-reduced-motion collapses every animation in Sections 15 and elsewhere to opacity-only equivalents.

Acceptance Criteria

"Done" for each screen, stated as a checklist an engineer or reviewer can literally tick against.

Home
  • Store status and ETA are server-rendered and visible with zero scroll, in both themes.
  • Your Usual appears above Best Sellers for any account with 2+ prior orders; is omitted (not empty) otherwise.
  • Late Night Mode re-ranks rows automatically past the configured evening threshold with no extra request.
  • LCP < 1.8s on a throttled 3G profile.
Search
  • Zero exact matches never renders a dead end — always falls back to nearest category or a message-the-store link.
  • Common typos and synonyms in the hand-curated map resolve to the correct product.
  • Every zero-match query is logged and visible in the owner console.
Cart
  • Free-delivery progress bar recalculates instantly and disappears (replaced by a confirmation pill) once crossed.
  • Remove always passes through a 4-second Undo state before permanent deletion.
  • Persists across a closed tab/app background for the same day.
Checkout
  • Only room number and phone are required fields; no other address field exists anywhere in the flow.
  • Delivery fee and free-delivery threshold shown match exactly what was shown in Cart, with no last-step change.
  • All validation is inline, on blur, field-specific.
Payment
  • QR enlarges to near-fullscreen on tap; UPI ID, number, and amount each have independent, working copy buttons with visible confirmation.
  • Screenshot upload is required to proceed and shows an immediate thumbnail preview.
  • Awaiting Verification shows a live, real (not fixed) estimated confirmation time and transitions automatically via realtime push — no manual refresh required.
  • A rejection/mismatch state gives a blame-free explanation and a direct re-upload path.
Order Tracking
  • All four stages (Payment Submitted → Confirmed → Coming → Delivered) are individually timestamped and persist across sessions.
  • A push notification fires on every stage change.
  • Cancellation always shows a stated reason and a visible refund-status note.
Cross-cutting
  • Every screen passes WCAG 2.1 AA contrast checks independently in light and dark themes.
  • No animation anywhere in the product exceeds 250ms or ignores prefers-reduced-motion.
  • No recommendation surface anywhere ships without a visible, true, disclosed reason.
