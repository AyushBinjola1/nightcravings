Owner Console Specification — Phase 3 · v1.0

The operating system NightCravings runs on, every night.

Builds on Foundation Doc v1.0 and Customer Experience Spec v1.0. Where the customer app optimizes for a 30-second order, this console optimizes for the owner's side of that same ten minutes — verifying, packing, and dispatching without ever putting the phone down mid-task.

Depends on Phase 1 + Phase 2 Status Draft for build Date 11 Jul 2026

Self-Review — bottlenecks found, workflows redesigned

The brief's own structure (separate Order Queue and Payment Verification sections; a sortable inventory table; role management as an afterthought) was tested against real hostel-shop operating conditions before writing the spec below. Five decisions changed as a result.

Risk
A separate Payment Verification screen creates a context switch the owner can't afford mid-rush. If verifying payment means leaving the order queue, every verification costs an extra navigation during the exact window (8–15 orders stacked at 11pm) when speed matters most.
Redesign
Payment verification is now inline on every order card in the Queue (§2) — screenshot, amount, and one-tap Approve/Reject live where the order already is. §3 becomes an aggregate Payment Triage view used only to clear a backlog in bulk, not the primary verification path.
Risk
A flat, sortable order table (as literally requested) optimizes for browsing, not triage. The owner never needs to sort orders — they need to know, at a glance, which single order has been waiting longest.
Redesign
The queue is a three-lane stack (Awaiting Verification → Preparing → Out for Delivery), each lane auto-sorted oldest-first, with the single oldest card visually weighted heavier. No manual sort control exists because there's nothing correct to sort by except wait time, which is already the default.
Risk
Payment fraud surface: nothing in the brief's requested fields stops a customer from re-submitting the same screenshot for a second order, or two customers from coincidentally uploading visually similar UPI app screenshots.
Redesign
Added automatic duplicate-screenshot detection (perceptual hash against the last 14 days of approved screenshots) — a visual "⚠ matches a screenshot already used for order #—" warning surfaces on the card before the owner taps Approve, turning a hard-to-catch fraud vector into a one-glance check.
Risk
Single point of failure: the entire operation lives on one owner's one device. A dropped phone, dead battery, or a night the owner is genuinely asleep stops 100% of order fulfillment with no fallback.
Redesign
Console ships as an installable PWA on any number of simultaneous devices with realtime sync (§18) — a friend, roommate, or the future first hire (§12, Delivery Partner role) can pick up a second phone and start clearing the queue with zero handoff ceremony, because there's no "who's logged in as admin" bottleneck, only role-scoped permissions.
Risk
Low-stock alerts based on a fixed threshold (e.g. "alert at 5 units") are wrong for two SKUs selling at very different velocities — 5 units of a slow mover is fine for a week; 5 units of the best-selling energy drink at 11pm might not survive the next hour.
Redesign
Low stock (§4) is computed from a velocity model — units remaining ÷ recent hourly sell-through rate — surfaced as "≈40 min of stock left at tonight's pace," not a static count. This is the single highest-leverage change in this document for the "reduce inventory mistakes" goal in the brief.
Console shell — a command-palette-first layout, not a sidebar-of-forty-links. ⌘K opens search-everything from any screen; the persistent left rail carries only the six screens used every single night.

Dashboard

A two-second answer to "is anything on fire," not a reporting surface.

Business reasoning

Every widget maps to one of the brief's eight goals; a widget that's purely informational without an action (e.g. a static "total customers" counter) was cut in favor of ones with a next step attached.

Widget: Pending Orders

Rendered in warning color specifically because it's the one tile that should visually nag — tapping it jumps straight into the Order Queue, oldest-first.

Widget: Low Stock Items

A compact list (not a tile) of the 3–5 nearest-to-empty SKUs by the velocity model from §4, each with a one-tap "Mark restocked" action right there — no need to leave the dashboard for a quick top-up entry.

Widget: Store Status

A single large toggle at the very top — Open/Closed — because this is the one control the owner reaches for constantly (stepping out, running low on an item, closing early). Everything else on the dashboard is secondary to this.

Widget: Revenue Graph

A 24-hour sparkline, not a full chart — shows tonight's shape (the two nightly peaks) at a glance; tapping it opens the full Analytics view (§7) for anyone who wants to go deeper.

Widget: Top Selling Products (today)

A ranked mini-list of 5, existing purely to inform tomorrow's restock conversation with the supplier — not a chart, just names and counts.

Widget: Quick Actions

Three buttons only — Add Product, Mark Closed, Message Support — the three actions an owner reaches for outside the normal order flow.

Widget: Recent Activity

A compact timeline (last 10 events: orders, verifications, stock edits) — mainly a "what just happened while I was packing" recovery tool, not a permanent audit log (that's §12).

Layout

Store Status full-width at top; stat tiles in a responsive grid below; Low Stock and Recent Activity as two side-by-side lists on desktop, stacked on mobile.

Loading

Tiles skeleton at their exact final size; Store Status renders instantly from cached state, then confirms via realtime subscription.

Empty state

Day-one dashboard with zero orders shows tiles at "—" rather than "0," with a single prompt: "Share your NightCravings link with the hostel WhatsApp group."

Performance

All tiles are realtime-subscribed (Supabase channel), not polled — a new order updates the Pending tile within a second, without a refresh.

02

Live Order Queue — the most important screen

Three lanes, not a table: Awaiting Verification, Preparing, Out for Delivery. Each card carries everything needed to act without opening anything else. On mobile the three lanes become three swipeable tabs with live counts; on desktop they sit side by side, Linear-board style.

Order Card

Everything needed to move an order forward, with zero secondary navigation.

Fields shown

Order ID, customer name, room + block, order time (relative, "3 min ago"), value, items with quantity, notes, delivery method, payment status, transaction ID, ETA — all on one card, no expand needed for the common case.

Progressive disclosure

Payment screenshot, phone number, and full order history for this customer sit one tap behind "View Screenshot" / the customer name — kept off the default card face so the lane doesn't turn into a wall of images.

Actions — Awaiting Verification lane

Approve Payment, Reject, View Screenshot, Call, WhatsApp — Approve is the sole primary (filled) button; every other action is secondary.

Actions — Preparing lane

Mark Delivery Coming (primary), Reject/Cancel, Call, WhatsApp — no "Verify Payment" clutter since that's already resolved by this lane.

Actions — Out for Delivery lane

Mark Delivered (primary, large) — a single dominant action, since this is the card the owner taps while physically walking, often one-handed.

Interactions

Swipe-right on any card advances it to the next lane (mirrors the primary button, for one-handed use while carrying a packed bag); swipe-left opens a quick "flag issue" sheet (out of stock / customer unreachable).

Animations

A card moving lanes slides and settles into its new lane's sort position (oldest-first) over 200ms; the moment Approve is tapped, the payment pill cross-fades from amber to green instantly (optimistic), before server confirmation returns.

Loading / optimism

Every stage-change button applies its effect to the UI immediately (optimistic update) and reconciles silently in the background; a failed sync rolls the card back with a toast — the owner is never staring at a spinner mid-rush.

Sort

Fixed oldest-first within each lane — no manual sort control exists, because "longest-waiting" is the only ordering that's ever correct here.

Urgency weighting

A card waiting past 2× the store's average stage time gets a thin red left-edge — not a badge to read, a peripheral-vision signal while scanning the lane.

Empty state

A lane with zero cards shows a quiet single line ("All caught up") rather than an empty box — reads as a status, not a broken screen.

Error states

A stage-change that fails to sync shows an inline "Couldn't update — retrying" on the card itself, with automatic retry, rather than a modal interrupting the whole queue.

Responsive

Mobile: one lane visible at a time behind swipeable tabs with live lane counts in the tab label. Desktop: all three lanes visible side-by-side, keyboard-navigable.

Accessibility

Each lane is a labeled region; cards are keyboard-focusable with the primary action bound to Enter, and lane-count changes announced via a polite live region.

Performance

Realtime-subscribed; new orders animate into Awaiting Verification without a page refresh, ever.

Future scalability

Multi-hostel adds a lane-level hostel filter chip, not a new screen; a Delivery Partner role (§12) sees only the Out for Delivery lane.

03

Payment Verification

Per the redesign above, verification itself lives on the order card (§2). This screen is the triage view for two situations the inline flow doesn't cover: clearing a backlog after being away from the phone, and investigating a specific disputed or flagged payment in detail.

Payment Triage

Clear a backlog fast, and investigate any flagged payment with full detail.

Payment queue

Same cards as the Awaiting Verification lane, but here shown as a dense list (screenshot thumbnail, amount, name, room) for rapid scanning when there are 10+ backed up.

Screenshot viewer

Full-screen zoomable image with the claimed amount and UTR overlaid as a fixed header, so the owner never has to hold two numbers in their head while comparing.

Duplicate detection

Perceptual-hash match against the last 14 days of approved screenshots; a match surfaces a red banner — "Matches a screenshot approved for order #NC-1799" — directly on the card, before Approve is even reachable.

Wrong amount

If the entered order total and a manually-read screenshot amount don't match, a "Partial / Mismatched Payment" action opens a resolution sheet: request a top-up, refund the difference, or approve with a logged override reason.

Fraud prevention

Beyond duplicate-hash detection: a running per-phone-number flag count is visible on the customer's mini-profile (see §6) — three or more rejected/mismatched payments surfaces a quiet "review before approving" note, never an automatic ban.

Verification history

Every approve/reject is timestamped with the owner's action and (if applicable) override reason — the audit trail this section exists to produce.

Approve / Reject

Both are single-tap from the triage list; Reject requires selecting a reason (wrong amount / duplicate / unreadable / other) from a short list — never a free-text requirement that slows down a rush.

Audit trail

Exportable (CSV, §10) — every payment decision ever made, attributable to a specific owner/staff account once Role Management (§12) is live.

04

Inventory

Modeled on Shopify's inventory table because that density and inline-edit speed is genuinely the right tool here — but restock, the single most common action, gets its own dedicated one-tap gesture instead of living inside a full edit form.

Inventory Table

See true stock health at a glance; fix the common cases in one tap.

Columns

Image, name, category, price, cost price, profit (computed, not entered), stock, status pill, supplier, expiry (if applicable).

Low stock model

Not a fixed threshold — "≈40 min of stock left at tonight's pace," computed from recent hourly sell-through; the status pill reads Low only when that projected runway drops under a configurable window (default 2 hours).

Quick restock

A stepper (− / qty / +) inline on the row, plus a "+10 / +24 / +48" quick-add chip set matching common case-pack sizes — restocking after a supplier drop-off is a 2-second action, never a full edit form.

Inline edit

Price and stock are directly editable in the cell (click, type, blur-to-save); every other field opens the full Product panel (§5).

Search / filter / sort

Search-as-you-type across name and category; filter chips for Low Stock / Out of Stock / Category; sort by any column header, defaulting to "stock runway ascending" so the most urgent restock is always row one.

Bulk actions

Multi-select via checkbox → bulk category change, bulk hide, bulk price adjustment (± amount or %) — for the seasonal "everything +₹2" adjustment.

Stock history / logs

Every stock change (sale, restock, manual correction) timestamped per product — the ground truth for "why does the count not match what's on the shelf."

Image upload

Drag-drop or camera capture directly from the row's expand — real photography is a brand requirement (Phase 2 §4), so this is a first-class, fast action, not buried in a settings tab.

Virtualized rendering

Table virtualizes rows beyond ~50 items — stays instant even as the catalogue grows toward multi-hostel scale.

Empty state

Zero products (day one): a single "Add your first product" call to action replaces the table, not an empty grid with headers and nothing under them.

Future scalability

A hostel/location column appears automatically the moment a second hostel exists — schema already scoped by hostelId from the Foundation Doc's architecture, no migration required.

05

Product Management

Add/Edit never leaves the Inventory table's context — it opens as a slide-over panel over the table (Shopify/Linear convention), so the owner's filtered view and scroll position are exactly as they left them on close.

ActionBehaviour
Add ProductSlide-over with Name, Category, Price, Cost Price, Image, Description as the only required fields — everything else (nutrition, tags, synonyms) sits under a collapsed "More details" to keep the fast path fast.
Edit ProductSame panel, pre-filled; autosave on blur per field rather than a single "Save" gate at the bottom, so a half-finished edit is never lost.
Duplicate ProductOne action from the row menu — clones every field except name (suffixed " copy") and stock (reset to 0) — the fast path for near-identical variants (flavours, pack sizes).
Hide ProductA toggle, not a delete — hidden products stay in Inventory and Analytics history but disappear from the customer catalogue instantly (realtime).
Delete ProductReserved for genuine mistakes (wrong entry); requires typing the product name to confirm, and is blocked outright if the product has order history — history is preserved by hiding, never destroyed.
Schedule ProductAn optional "Available from / until" pair of times — for a genuinely time-boxed item (e.g. a breakfast-only combo) — becomes visible/orderable automatically at that window, no manual toggle needed at 7am.
Search keywords / synonymsA tag-input field feeding directly into the Phase 2 §2 search synonym map — this is the one place the owner directly improves customer-facing search quality.
06

Customers

Not a CRM in the enterprise sense — a lightweight profile that answers "who is this person, and have I seen a problem with them before" in one glance, since the owner personally knows most of these residents.

Customer Profile

One glance at relationship and reliability, before answering a call.

Header

Name, phone (tap-to-call), room/hostel, a small reliability note if a payment-flag count exists (§3) — surfaced quietly, not as a scarlet badge.

Stats

Total orders, total spend, average order value, favourite products (top 3 by frequency) — all computed, none manually entered.

Order timeline

Every past order as a compact reverse-chronological list, each expandable to the full order card from §2's history.

Support history

Any WhatsApp/call notes the owner has logged against this customer — freeform, optional, exists mainly for handoff once a second staff member is helping (§12).

Search / sort

Search by name, phone, or room; sort by total spend or most recent order — the two views an owner actually needs ("who are my best customers" and "who ordered recently").

Future: segmentation

Reserved list view for cohorts (e.g. "ordered 3+ times but not in 14 days" — a lapsing-customer segment) once order volume justifies proactive outreach; not built until that volume exists.

07

Analytics

Charts here use a validated, CVD-safe chart palette kept deliberately separate from the brand's amber/indigo UI tokens (mixing brand hues into a categorical chart palette fails contrast and color-blind-safety checks) — brand color stays in the chrome, chart color stays in the chart tokens defined above.

Analytics

Turn nightly operations into decisions about stocking, pricing, and timing.

Revenue / Orders / Profit

A single line chart per metric with a comparison toggle (this week vs. last week) — one axis, one hue (--chart-1), never a dual-axis overlay of two different-scale metrics.

Peak hours heatmap

Hour-of-day × day-of-week grid, sequential single-hue ramp (light→dark blue) — reveals the two nightly humps (post-dinner, post-midnight) that directly inform staffing a second device.

Top / worst products

Two ranked bar lists side by side, sharing one categorical palette in fixed slot order — worst-sellers view exists specifically to inform what to drop from the catalogue, not just what to promote.

Margins & most profitable category

A composition view (stacked bar by category, 2px gaps between segments) showing revenue vs. margin contribution — surfaces when a popular product is actually low-margin.

Repeat customers / retention

A simple cohort-style retention curve — % of customers still ordering at week 2, 4, 8 — the single number that answers "is this a real business or a novelty."

Avg delivery / verification time

Two sparklines with a plain-language trend line ("verification time down 40s this week") — direct feedback on whether the owner's own operational speed is improving.

Insights strip

2–3 auto-generated, plainly-worded observations above the charts ("Thursdays are your slowest night" / "Sting Energy sells out by 1am twice a week") — insight-first, chart-second, since the owner shouldn't have to derive the finding themselves.

Accessibility

Every chart ships a table-view toggle; legends are always present for 2+ series; status/trend colors (good/warn) always pair with an icon and label, never color alone.

Category Management

Categories

Keep the customer-facing shelf exactly as flat and scannable as Phase 2's IA requires.

Create / edit / delete

Name, icon, cover image — deleting a non-empty category prompts a required reassignment of its products, never a silent orphan.

Reorder

Drag-to-reorder list directly controls the order of category tiles on customer Home — what the owner sees here is what customers see, no separate "publish" step.

Hide

Same pattern as Hide Product — removed from customer view, retained in the console and in historical analytics.

Future: nested categories

Deliberately not built — Phase 1's IA is intentionally flat, and nesting would be solving a problem this catalogue size doesn't have. Revisit only if multi-hostel catalogues genuinely grow past a few hundred SKUs per location.

Workflows

The full state machine behind every order, with every decision point and branch made explicit.

Customer places order → Delivered (happy path)
  1. Order placed. Card appears in Awaiting Verification with an audible chime + push notification. Optimistically visible within <1s of the customer's submit.
  2. Owner opens screenshot inline (no navigation), compares the copied amount, checks the duplicate-hash banner (absent = clean).
  3. Owner taps Approve Payment. Card's payment pill flips to green instantly (optimistic); customer's Verification Heartbeat (Phase 2 §8) resolves to Confirmed in the same moment via realtime push both ways.
  4. Card auto-moves to Preparing. Owner physically packs the order; taps Mark Delivery Coming when leaving the pantry/room.
  5. Card moves to Out for Delivery. Customer sees the corresponding stage (Phase 2 §9) with a live estimate.
  6. Owner taps Mark Delivered upon handoff. Card exits the board into Order History; customer's app shows Delivered and the order becomes reorderable.
Decision point: payment doesn't match

Owner taps Reject instead of Approve → required reason (wrong amount / duplicate / unreadable / other) → customer's app immediately shows the specific, blame-free explanation from Phase 2 §17, with a direct re-upload path. No order progresses past Awaiting Verification without an explicit Approve.

Decision point: item turns out to be out of stock mid-preparation

Owner swipe-left → flag issue → "Item unavailable" → selects the affected line item → system auto-computes a partial refund or substitution offer, sent to the customer as a one-tap accept/decline before the order proceeds — never silently dropped from the order.

Decision point: customer unreachable at delivery

After two Call attempts (logged automatically via the Call button's tap event), owner can mark "Attempted — Unreachable," which pauses the order in Out for Delivery with a visible timer rather than forcing an immediate Delivered/Cancelled decision — gives a real grace window before escalating to a refund conversation.

Edge Cases

CaseResolution
Customer pays wrong amountFlagged automatically if entered total ≠ order total; Reject with "wrong amount" reason triggers the customer-side correction flow (Phase 2 §17).
Customer pays twiceSecond screenshot's perceptual hash won't match (it's a genuinely new payment) but the order ID context makes it visible on the customer's order list; owner issues a one-tap refund from that order's card, logged in the audit trail.
Wrong / unreadable screenshotReject with "unreadable" reason; customer gets a direct re-upload prompt, order stays parked in Awaiting Verification (not cancelled) so no re-ordering is needed.
Product goes out of stock after order placedSee Workflow above — substitution/partial-refund offer, never a silent removal.
Owner rejects the whole orderRequired reason, customer sees an explicit "Order Cancelled" stage with refund status (Phase 2 §9) — never a disappearance.
Store closes mid-queueExisting Awaiting Verification / Preparing / Out for Delivery cards are entirely unaffected — closing only stops new orders, per Phase 2 §11's closed-but-browsable model.
Network failure on the owner's deviceConsole is a PWA with offline queue caching — actions taken offline (e.g. Mark Delivered) queue locally and sync the moment connectivity returns, never silently lost.
Payment verification timeoutPast the estimate window, both the owner's card and the customer's Heartbeat surface an escalation path simultaneously (§11), rather than either side silently waiting.
Duplicate order (same customer, same items, seconds apart — likely a double-tap)System detects two orders from the same phone number within a 60-second window with identical contents and surfaces a merge suggestion to the owner before both are prepared separately.
RefundOne-tap from any order's card at any stage; requires a reason, always logged, and reflected instantly on the customer's order history as a distinct "Refunded" status.
17

Accessibility

  • WCAG 2.1 AA contrast across both themes, verified independently — status pills always pair color with an icon and text label.
  • Full keyboard operability across the Queue, Inventory table, and all panels — every primary action reachable without a pointer, essential for the desktop/laptop packing-adjacent workflow.
  • Screen-reader users get each order card announced as one coherent unit (customer, items, value, status) rather than fragmented cells.
  • Live regions announce lane-count and stage changes as they happen in realtime, matching the visual update.
  • Minimum 44×44px targets throughout, sized up further (48px+) on the mobile Queue given the one-handed, hands-full usage context.
18

Performance

  • Realtime everywhere it matters — Order Queue, Dashboard tiles, and Notifications are Supabase-realtime subscribed; nothing operationally relevant is polled.
  • Optimistic UI on every stage-change and payment-decision action — the interface updates instantly and reconciles in the background, with silent rollback + toast on failure.
  • Virtualized tables for Inventory and Order History beyond ~50 rows — stays instant at multi-hostel scale.
  • Infinite scroll on Order History and Customers, never full pagination — matches the "keep scanning, don't click Next" scanning behaviour of both screens.
  • Lazy-loaded Analytics charts and the Reports export pipeline — neither blocks the Queue's initial paint, which is the one screen that must load first, always.
  • Multi-device by default — no single-session lock; two devices open to the Queue simultaneously both see and can act on the same realtime state, directly addressing the single-point-of-failure risk raised in the Self-Review.
19

Acceptance Criteria

Order Queue
  • A new order appears in Awaiting Verification within 1 second of submission, with an audible + push alert.
  • Approve/Reject, Mark Delivery Coming, and Mark Delivered all apply optimistically and never block on network round-trip.
  • Cards within each lane are always sorted oldest-first with no manual override control present.
  • A duplicate-screenshot match is flagged before Approve is actionable.
Inventory
  • Low-stock status reflects the velocity model (runway time), not a fixed unit threshold.
  • Restock via the inline stepper/quick-add updates stock count and status pill with zero full-page reload.
  • Every stock change is recorded in Stock History, attributable to sale vs. manual edit.
Analytics
  • All charts use the validated chart-token palette, never brand UI hues, and pass CVD/contrast checks in both themes.
  • Every multi-series chart ships a table-view toggle.
  • No chart uses a dual y-axis; differently-scaled metrics are always separate charts or indexed to a common base.
Cross-cutting
  • Every screen in this document is fully keyboard-operable and passes WCAG 2.1 AA in both themes.
  • Every owner action that changes money, stock, or order state is logged to the audit trail with actor, timestamp, and reason where applicable.
  • The console functions correctly as an installable PWA on at least two simultaneous devices with realtime-consistent state.
